INTUITIVE GUIDE TO ABCD-16 REPLICATION
=======================================


PEDIGREE
--------
- A family tree, but drawn for medical genetics purposes
- Shows who is related to whom, who is tested, who carries a mutation
- Example: grandparents -> parents -> children, connected by lines


CARRIER
-------
- A person who has the disease-causing gene mutation
- May or may not be sick themselves (some mutations only cause disease if you inherit two copies)
- Key insight: if your parent is a carrier, you have a 50% chance of being one too
- So testing one person updates your beliefs about untested relatives


BELIEF STATE (s)
----------------
- At any point in the testing process, you don't know for certain who is a carrier
- The belief state = your current probability distribution over who might be a carrier
- As you test people, you update these probabilities (Bayesian updating)
- c_i(s) = probability person i is a carrier given what you know so far


FRONTIER
--------
- The set of untested people who are still decision-relevant
- Testing them would update your beliefs in a meaningful way
- People deep in already-resolved branches fall off the frontier


BRIDGE / ARTICULATION NODE
---------------------------
- A person in the pedigree who connects two otherwise separate branches
- Example: a parent connects their siblings' branch to their children's branch
- Testing a bridge person gives you information about multiple branches at once -> high value


DEPTH
-----
- How far down in the family tree a person is
- Testing someone deeper costs the same but often gives less downstream value (fewer untested descendants below them)


MYOPIC POLICY
-------------
- The greedy one-step-ahead strategy: "which action (test someone / stop) looks best right now, ignoring future consequences?"
- Often good but not optimal -- it can miss that testing person A now unlocks valuable information about persons B, C, D later


V* (EXACT OPTIMUM)
-------------------
- The true optimal expected value of the best possible sequential testing strategy
- Computed by exact DP -- expensive but provably correct


V_STOP (STOP-ONLY VALUE)
-------------------------
- What you get if you stop testing immediately and never test anyone
- This is the baseline / floor -- any testing policy should beat this


U (ADP UPPER BOUND)
--------------------
- The ADP model gives you a certificate that the true optimum V* is at most U
- Comes from the LP relaxation -- it's an upper bound on what's achievable


L (POLICY LOWER BOUND)
-----------------------
- The value you actually get by running the ADP policy
- It's a lower bound on V* -- proves you can achieve at least this much


THE GAP INTUITION
-----------------

    V_stop  <=  L  <=  V*  <=  U
     floor    policy  optimal  certificate

- denom  = V* - V_stop        = total room for improvement over doing nothing
- ratio2 = (V* - L) / denom   = how much of that room your policy FAILS to capture  (lower = better)
- ratio3 = (U - V*) / denom   = how loose your certificate is above optimal          (lower = better)
- Both should be close to 0


WHAT IS THIS PROBLEM?
---------------------
- A family has a hereditary genetic disease. You need to decide who in the family to test for a genetic mutation.
- Testing costs money. Finding carriers has value (informs treatment / family planning).
- The pedigree creates information dependencies: testing one person updates your beliefs about their relatives.
- Goal: find the optimal sequential testing policy -- which person to test next, or when to stop.


WHY IS IT HARD?
---------------
- Exact DP gives the provably optimal answer but is computationally intractable for large families.
- So you use ADP (Approximate Dynamic Programming) -- a linear function approximation over hand-crafted features of the belief state.


WHAT IS ABCD-16?
----------------
- An ADP model with 16 fixed features describing the current belief state of the pedigree.
- 13 structural/myopic features (A/D block):
    - how much carrier risk is on the frontier of untested people
    - bridge structure, sibling breadth
    - marginal gain of testing next vs stopping
- 3 regime features (B/C block):
    - broader statistical summaries of all untested people and allele-asymmetry effects
- These features go into a linear LP:
    Phi(s) = base(s) + sum_j  beta_j * feature_j(s)
- The LP coefficients beta_j are learned by solving an ALP (Approximate LP)


WHAT DOES THIS REPO DO?
------------------------
- It is a replication package: re-runs the ABCD-16 solver from scratch on 4 sample families
  (or all 102) and verifies the published ratio2/ratio3 numbers match to within 1e-6.
- Uses Gurobi as the LP solver.
- Guarantees freshness: no disk caching across runs, every invocation recomputes from scratch.
- All 4 sample rows pass with diff = +0.00e+00 (exact reproduction).


DATA / FAMILIES
---------------
- 102 families total across 3 suites: original8, local40, phase6_54
- Each family = a pedigree topology + reward preset + allele frequencies + cost structure
- The 4-sample fast audit covers:
    - two topology types    (Extended, ThreeGeneration)
    - two allele regimes    (LowHigh, MediumEven)
    - two cost presets      (Base, Aggressive)
    - best-case and worst-case rows
